Layout Archetype · Editorial-Narrative Family

Cover & Contents: A Formal Opening Before the Argument Begins

“A cover promises weight; the page that follows has to prove it.”

Two committed pages, not one continuous scroll: a full-bleed cover isolates a single title and lets it own the whole page, uninterrupted by anything else. Immediately beneath it, a numbered contents index proves the weight was earned — that pairing, cover then index, is the entire mechanism.

When to Reach for This Archetype 03
Formal reports, board decks, research monographs, RFP responses — anything whose reader expects a title page and an index, because the alternative reads as unfinished.
02
When Not To 04
Skip it for informal memos, one-pagers, or a quick internal brief — pairing a full ceremonial cover with a numbered TOC on a half-page document reads as bureaucratic throat-clearing. Use a plain heading, or magazine-well's masthead, instead.
03
The Structural Rule 05
Page two is a numbered index, not a second cover. The moment its entries get boxed, shadowed, or turned into cards, it has drifted into the uniform-card-grid look this skill exists to prevent — keep the list a list.
04
Editorial Devices This Archetype Favors 06
Dot-leader numbering, a literal folio-marked seam between the two pages, and restrained page-two typography — page two intentionally under-performs page one.
