Analyst Register · Not A Dashboard

Every Number Earns a Figure. No Number Floats Alone.

The concept: a straight vertical sequence of numbered, captioned exhibits, each carrying a short paragraph of interpretation — the register of an analyst note or quarterly review, not a stat-tile dashboard.

There is no lead here, and no well. Structurally this archetype is a single stream: masthead, a short framing brief, then four to six exhibits running down the page in sequence, each one identical in its parts — a figure number, a one-line caption, the visual itself, and a commentary paragraph — even as their content and size vary. The exhibits are peers, not a hero-and-supporting-cast arrangement; what gives the page hierarchy is the discipline of the numbering, not a size contrast between a "top" item and everything else.

"A chart with no caption and no sentence of interpretation is a KPI tile wearing a report's clothing. Give it a number, a caption, and one sentence of what it means, or leave it out."

Use this when your content is a sequence of findings that need to be shown, not just argued: analyst notes, quarterly business reviews, research summaries, investor letters, board memos with supporting data. If someone might reasonably ask "which exhibit are you talking about?", this archetype is doing its job.

FIG. 01 — Anatomy of one exhibit: number, caption, visual, commentary.

Every exhibit in this archetype carries all four parts, every time — not as a style preference, as the structural rule that keeps this from turning into a dashboard. The number is what makes an exhibit citable ("see Fig. 3") instead of just another chart floating on the page.

Structural rule, not a style preference — see this preset's own antiPatternCheck.notes.

FIG. 02 — Where this archetype earns its keep.

Analyst notes, quarterly business reviews, research summaries, investor letters — anything organized around a sequence of findings that need to be shown, cited, and interpreted rather than just asserted in prose.

FIG. 03 — When not to reach for this archetype.

If the content needs live filtering, drill-down, or per-user navigation — not a fixed sequence a reader moves through top to bottom — that's dashboard-with-rail, not this archetype. This archetype produces a document; a dashboard produces an instrument a user operates.

FIG. 04 — The rule that prevents dashboard drift.

Every exhibit is numbered, captioned, and interpreted — no exceptions, no matter how self-explanatory a chart feels in the moment. A bare stat with no caption and no sentence of commentary is the anti-pattern this whole archetype exists to replace.

FIG. 05 — Editorial devices this archetype favors.

One pull quote per report, not per exhibit. Sidenotes for citations, attached to the specific exhibit they source — never a persistent rail. Section breaks are the figure numbers themselves; there's no separate rule or ornament marking a new part of the report.

Sidenote convention demonstrated here, attached directly beneath the exhibit it cites — exactly where a reader needs it, not collected in a rail or endnote list.

Internal Memo · Support Operations

Why the Support Queue Backlog Stopped Shrinking

Three straight months of headcount growth and the backlog held flat instead of falling — the number that mattered wasn't the queue size, it was the one exhibit below.

Since March, the support team grew from eleven to sixteen agents against a hiring plan built to clear the backlog by June. Headcount arrived on schedule. The backlog didn't move. The obvious read — agents ramping slower than planned — turned out to be wrong once we split ticket volume by channel instead of by agent.

"New agents were fully productive within three weeks. The backlog was flat because the queue kept growing, not because it stopped shrinking."

The one exhibit below explains where the additional volume came from. Everything else in this memo is context for reading it correctly, not additional data to track.

FIG. 01 — Ticket volume by channel, January–June.

Chat volume nearly doubled after the March pricing-page redesign added a chat widget to every plan-comparison view; email and phone volume held flat across the same period. The backlog isn't a throughput problem, it's a channel-mix problem the hiring plan didn't anticipate.

Source: support platform export, deduplicated by ticket thread, not by message count.

Analyst Note · Product Analytics

The May Release Didn't Break Checkout — It Moved Where People Drop Off

Five exhibits, in sequence, walk from the top-line conversion number down to the specific step causing it — no single chart tells the whole story alone.

Checkout conversion fell 1.8 points in the two weeks after the May 14 release, enough to trigger a rollback review. The exhibits below are the actual sequence the on-call analyst worked through to find the cause, in the order they were pulled — not reordered for narrative effect.

FIG. 01 — Checkout conversion rate, daily, April 1–May 28.

The drop is real and it's step-function, not gradual — conversion holds steady through May 13, then drops and stays down starting May 14, the release date. That rules out a slow external cause like a seasonal dip.

FIG. 02 — Conversion by funnel step, pre- vs. post-release.

Steps 1 and 2 (cart review, address entry) are unchanged. Step 3, payment method selection, lost 4.2 points of completion — the entire top-line drop is attributable to one step.

Funnel steps defined per the shared checkout instrumentation spec, v3.

FIG. 03 — Payment method mix, pre- vs. post-release.

Saved-card usage fell six points and new-card entry rose by the same amount — the May 14 release changed which payment option loads as the pre-selected default, and most users don't change a pre-selected option.

FIG. 04 — New-card entry error rate, by field.

New-card entry has a materially higher error rate on the CVV field than saved-card checkout ever showed, which explains why the default switch cost conversion instead of being neutral.

FIG. 05 — Projected conversion recovery if the pre-selected default reverts.

Reverting the default should recover roughly 3.8 of the 4.2 points lost at step 3; the remaining gap is attributed to the CVV error rate, a separate fix already in the sprint.

Quarterly Review · Infrastructure Spend

Vendor Spend Review, Q2 — Where the Increase Actually Went

Twelve vendors, three exhibits — comparable line items are grouped into ranked tables, not given one card each.

Total infrastructure spend rose 14% quarter over quarter. Below is where it went, grouped by category rather than itemized vendor by vendor — a twelve-card grid would flatten the one distinction that matters: which increases were volume-driven and which were rate-driven.

FIG. 01 — Top five vendors by quarter-over-quarter spend increase, ranked.

Compute and observability account for four of the top five increases; only one, the CDN contract, is a rate change rather than a usage change — worth flagging separately below since it's the only line item that needs a renegotiation, not a usage review.

FIG. 02 — CDN contract: rate vs. usage decomposition.

Usage grew 3%. The bill grew 22%. The gap is entirely the January rate-card change taking effect this quarter — this is a procurement conversation, not an engineering one.
