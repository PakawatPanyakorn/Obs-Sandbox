Magazine Well: One Story Owns the Page, the Rest Support It

The concept: give one piece of content full visual dominance through size and isolation, then let supporting content pool beneath it at clearly reduced weight. The size contrast alone communicates importance — no color, box, or badge required.

Structurally, this is a two-tier page: a single "lead" region running full width with the largest type and the only large image, followed by a "well" — a band of shorter secondary entries sharing space beneath it. The well entries are visibly smaller and more numerous, which is what makes the lead read as the lead, not just the first item in a list.

"The lead isn't first in a sequence — it's the only thing at that scale on the page. That's what makes it the lead, not its position."

Use this when you have one clearly dominant story or finding and three to five genuinely secondary ones: a homepage with a flagship feature and supporting links, an annual report's headline result with supporting metrics, a newsletter's top story and a roundup beneath it, a case-study page with one hero outcome and supporting details.

When Not to Use This Archetype

Content where every item is genuinely equal in importance

A glossary, a directory, a reference list — nothing here should be forced into a "lead." Reach for card-catalogue-index or two-column-academic instead, where structure comes from alignment and rules, not size hierarchy.

Structural rule for the well

Well entries are separated by whitespace and a hairline rule only — never a border, shadow, or rounded box. Size may vary slightly with content length, but should never be forced perfectly uniform; that reintroduces the card-grid look this archetype exists to avoid.

Editorial devices this archetype favors

One pull quote in the lead, breaking the text column at its most quotable line. A caption under the lead image. No footnotes or sidenotes — this archetype reads as narrative, not reference material.

When the Retro Finally Changed Something

No image — with prose-only content, the lead's dominance comes entirely from type size and an opening paragraph none of the well entries get.

After eighteen months split across two time zones, the team's real problem surfaced in a retro that almost didn't happen: nobody agreed on what "done" meant for the same ticket. Engineering called it done at merge. Product called it done at release. Support called it done when the ticket stopped reopening. All three were right, and all three were talking past each other in every planning meeting since January.

"We didn't fix the disagreement. We just wrote down which 'done' we meant, every time, until it stopped being a disagreement."

The fix cost nothing — a one-line label on every ticket stating which definition applied. What it bought back was every planning meeting that used to relitigate the same argument.
