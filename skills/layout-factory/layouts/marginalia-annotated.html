The Manuscript and Its Gloss

marginalia-annotated pairs a running main text with an annotation column dense enough to hold a live argument — not sparse citations, a running commentary that talks back to the text beside it.

Structurally this is two parallel columns, not a lead followed by a well: a wide manuscript column carries the primary text at full sentence detail1, and a narrower gloss column beside it carries one commentary entry for every marked claim, clarification, or objection2 — including, as in this very paragraph, several stacked against one dense sentence3.

"A gloss column earns its width by disagreeing, clarifying, or questioning — not by citing. That's what separates it from a citation margin."

Use this when a document is under active review with a specific person's commentary attached to it: draft manuscript review, editorial peer review, a supervisor's notes on a thesis chapter, legal or contract redlining, a critical edition's gloss on an old source text.

Marginal Notes
1Challenge
"Full sentence detail" undersells the body column's job — it also carries the document's single pull quote, which this gloss column never touches.
2Clarify
"One entry per marked claim" is the median case, not a rule — nothing stops several stacked entries against a single paragraph, as the next mark shows.
3Agree
Agreed — this is the one structural fact that actually separates marginalia-annotated from tufte-sidenote, more than the color-coded labels do.
About This Archetype
When Not To Use This
Reach for tufte-sidenote instead when notes are citations or source references, one per paragraph, mostly empty margins — sparse by design. This archetype is for dense, argumentative commentary, not attribution.
Structural Rule
Every gloss entry anchors to a specific marked phrase in the body. An entry with no visible anchor is noise, not marginalia — and stacking notes just to fill the column is the same anti-pattern as a card grid with nothing to compare.
Editorial Devices Favored
Categorized labels (challenge / clarify / query / agree), one restrained pull quote for the author's own claim kept out of the annotation color, and hairline rules between entries — never boxes.

Why We Stopped Shipping Quarterly Roadmaps

Draft v3 — inline notes from the editor, keyed to the sentences below.

Quarterly roadmaps promised predictability1 and delivered theater instead2: a plan re-litigated every ninety days, signed by people who had stopped believing in it by day thirty.

We replaced it with a rolling six-week horizon3 and, more importantly, stopped asking anyone to commit past that horizon4.

"The horizon length was never the fix. The fix was refusing to make anyone promise what they couldn't yet see."
Editor's Notes — Draft v3
1Clarify
Promised to whom — leadership, or the teams executing it? Worth naming the audience; the complaint that follows is really about the second group.
2Challenge
This is the strongest claim in the piece and it's currently unsupported. Either cite the March survey or soften to "felt like theater to the three teams we talked to."
3Query
Is six weeks a considered choice, or just "shorter than a quarter"? A sentence on why six specifically would preempt the obvious question.
4Agree
This is the actual mechanism, not the horizon length — good that the piece gets here, though it arrives a sentence later than it should.

Retention Recovers After the Onboarding Rewrite — With One Caveat

Analyst draft under review — reviewer notes flag methodology questions on specific claims, not a caption on every line.

Fig. 1 — 12-week retention, pre/post onboarding rewrite.

Retention at week six rose from 41% to 53% after the rewrite shipped1, and the improvement holds across every acquisition channel we tracked2.

We attribute the lift to the rewrite and not to the concurrent pricing change3, since the pricing change shipped two weeks after the retention curve had already moved.

Reviewer Notes — Fig. 1
1Challenge
53% is the number after excluding the two cohorts hit by the payment-outage incident in the same window. That exclusion needs to be stated in the body text, not just here.
2Query
"Every channel we tracked" — did that include the referral channel onboarded in week 4 of this window? Sample size there is thin.
3Clarify
Worth stating this timeline explicitly in the body — "two weeks after" is doing most of the causal argument's work and is currently only defended here.

API Rate-Limit Proposal — Requirements Under Review

Each requirement is a plain list entry, hairline-separated — never a bordered card — with reviewer commentary in the rail beside the entries that raised questions.

REQ-1 — Default limit of 100 req/min per API key1

Applies per authenticated key unless otherwise noted in the client's service agreement.

REQ-2 — Burst allowance of 20 req/sec for 5 seconds2

Intended to absorb legitimate traffic spikes without a hard reject.

REQ-3 — 429 responses must include a Retry-After header

Value expressed in seconds, matching the remainder of the current limit window.

REQ-4 — Limit increases require a signed support ticket3

Standard increases capped at 5x default; anything beyond escalates to engineering review.

Reviewer Notes
1Query
Is 100/min a per-key or per-org ceiling? REQ-1 reads per-key, but the appendix diagram shows per-org.
2Challenge
20/sec burst on top of 100/min lets a client spend a whole minute's budget in the first five seconds. Confirm that's intended, not an oversight in the math.
3Clarify
"Signed support ticket" — signed by whom? Worth naming the approver role so this isn't ambiguous during an incident.
