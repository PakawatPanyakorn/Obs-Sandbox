Field Notes · On Structure

Every Block's Size Comes From the Photograph, Not a Decision

The concept: lay out a sequence of images where each block's width and height are recorded once, per image, from that image's own aspect ratio and its weight in the story — never from a shared grid formula applied uniformly.

This region — the one you're reading now — is deliberately the quietest thing on the page. No image, small type, a single opening paragraph. That's on purpose: the entry point isn't the title, it's the first exhibit below, which earns its size purely because its source photo is a wide panorama. A tall portrait crop elsewhere on this page is tall for the identical reason — it IS tall. Nobody decided it mattered more.

Plate 01Full 12-column span, short row height — a wide panoramic crop. This is the archetype's only hero treatment (shadow + gradient); everything below it stays a bare outlined rectangle on purpose.
Plate 02Narrow column, the tallest block on the page — a portrait crop. Tall because the photo is tall, not because it's "next most important."
Plate 03Same column width as Plate 02, noticeably shorter — a squarer crop sitting beside it in the packed grid.
Plate 04Shortest of the three narrow blocks — a low landscape crop. grid-auto-flow: dense lets it backfill next to its taller neighbors instead of leaving a gap.
"A block is wide because the photo is wide. That's the whole rule — and the only rule this archetype has."
Plate 05An 8-column block paired with a 4-column one at the same row height — a two-up spread, the same pairing a print photo essay would call a spread.
Plate 06Its companion — same height as Plate 05 so the pair reads as one deliberate row, not a coincidence.
Plate 07Closing exhibit — full width again, but taller than the opener, giving the sequence a deliberate final beat rather than just trailing off.
When Not to Use This Archetype

Content where size should signal importance, not shape

If a block being bigger is meant to say "this finding matters more" rather than "this photo is this shape," reach for bento-mosaic instead — same variable-cell look, opposite sizing logic.

The rule that prevents a card-grid slide

Column/row spans are recorded once per exhibit from its real aspect ratio and narrative beat. If every exhibit ends up the same shape, the archetype has quietly become a boring uniform grid and should be abandoned, not forced.

Editorial devices this archetype favors

A quote-break exhibit for commentary mid-sequence, plate-numbered cutlines under every image, and nothing else — no drop caps, no footnotes. This reads as a visual sequence, not reference material.

Notes From a Two-Week Walk Along the Coast Road

Prose carries most of this piece — the masonry thins to a handful of real photos, and several blocks are text-only, sized to what they actually say.

The road only exists on maps as a single line, but it's really three different surfaces depending on which decade the county last repaved it. That's the kind of detail that doesn't need a photo to land — so it doesn't get one.

Mapping Six Years of Coastal Erosion, County by County

Exhibits become maps and charts instead of photos — a wide exhibit here is wide because the underlying timeline needs the width, not for visual variety.

Three counties account for most of the measured retreat. The rest is noise within the margin of error, which the maps below make obvious in a way the raw table did not.

Six Projects, Six Real Photographs, Six Real Shapes

A portfolio index — each item keeps its own image at its own real aspect ratio rather than being cropped to a uniform thumbnail.

None of these six are more important than the others — this is the weakest-fit variant of the three, and it shows: without a genuine narrative sequence, the varying shapes just describe six different photographs, not six different weights.

Item 01Harbor Signal House — a wide-format architectural shot, client: Port Authority.
Item 02Stairwell Study — a tall interior crop, client: private residence.
Item 03Market Awning Series — a square-ish crop, self-initiated.
Item 04Foundry Floor — low landscape crop, client: Regional Museum.
Item 05Loading Dock at Dusk — matches Item 03/04's height only because that's this photo's actual shape.
Item 06Full-Width Panorama, Rail Yard — client: Transit Authority. Full width because the shot is a panorama, same rule as every other item here.
A Note on This Variant

Why this feels like the weak link

All six items are genuinely equal — a uniform card grid would serve a client index just as well.

When it does earn its keep

Only when the images are real and really do vary in shape — not stock photos forced square to fit a template.

The line not to cross

Don't use varying spans here to imply Item 01 matters more than Item 05 — that would misuse the archetype's own sizing signal.
