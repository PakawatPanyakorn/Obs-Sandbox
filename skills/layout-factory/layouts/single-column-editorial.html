Archetype Notes

Single-Column Editorial: One Measure, Held All the Way Down

No sidebar, no grid of boxes, no competing rail — every region on this page, including the ones explaining it, sits inside the same reading measure.

single-column-editorialeditorial-narrative family

The concept is almost embarrassingly simple: pick one column width somewhere between 65 and 75 characters, center it, and put everything — headline, byline, argument, quote, aside — inside that same measure. Hierarchy doesn't come from boxes, rails, or icon grids. It comes entirely from type scale and whitespace: a headline is bigger than a subhead, a subhead is bigger than body text, and the space above a subhead is larger than the space between two paragraphs. That's the whole toolkit, and the discipline is using nothing else.

This is markdown's native shape, which is not a coincidence — a reader scrolling a rendered .md file already experiences exactly this: one column, headings by size, paragraphs by gap. The archetype just names the convention and adds two devices markdown doesn't have on its own: a drop cap to mark where reading begins, and a margin sidenote for material that would otherwise force a footnote jump.

The measure is the whole argument. Widen it for one image and you've admitted the column was negotiable — and once it's negotiable, everything else in the layout starts negotiating too.

Use this when a single voice is making a sustained argument and the reader is expected to move through it start to finish: essays, opinion pieces, long-form journalism, executive briefs, narrative research reports, internal memos that need to actually be read rather than skimmed.

When Not to Use This Archetype

If the content is fundamentally a set of comparable items — pricing tiers, product specs, a roundup of five features — this archetype fights you. Forcing parallel items into sequential prose to preserve the single column hides the comparison a reader actually needs. Reach for magazine-well when one item deserves top billing over several supporting ones, or a grid/table archetype when every item is genuinely equal and meant to be scanned side by side.

The Structural Rule

The measure is inviolate. Nothing inside the column — an image, a table, a code block, a quote — is ever allowed to exceed it; the only content permitted to live outside the column is a sidenote, and a sidenote may only be a citation or a brief aside, never navigation, a related-posts widget, or an ad. That one rule is what keeps this archetype from decaying into a generic blog-with-sidebar-ads page: the margin is reserved, not available.

This is the same discipline print typography has enforced for centuries — a footnote lives in the margin because it's genuinely secondary, not because there was empty space to fill.

Editorial Devices It Favors

Drop caps mark the start of the argument — used once, on the opening paragraph only, never decoratively elsewhere. Margin sidenotes carry citations and asides without breaking the reading flow the way a footnote's page-jump does. What this archetype deliberately avoids: callout boxes, stat tiles, and pull-quote sidebars — anything with a border and a fill competes with the column for the reader's eye, which is exactly the kind of competition a single column exists to eliminate.

Field Notes

The Postmortem Nobody Wanted to Write

A single incident review, held to one page and one voice, turned out to matter more than the six-week project it was reviewing.

By the platform team5 min read

Three days after the outage, the postmortem draft sat untouched in a shared doc with one comment on it: "do we really need six pages for a twenty-minute blip?" The instinct to pad an incident review with charts, a timeline graphic, and a risk matrix is strong, and almost always wrong — the actual finding fit in four paragraphs, and burying it in exhibits would have guaranteed nobody read past the executive summary.

So the final version was one column, no charts: what happened, why the alert didn't fire for eleven minutes, what changed as a result. Nothing else.

We didn't need a better dashboard. We needed one paragraph that said, plainly, why the alert stayed silent — and the discipline to stop there.

The eleven-minute gap turned out to be a permissions change made two sprints earlier, unrelated to the incident it was first blamed for.

"Unrelated" took four days to confirm. The postmortem doesn't show that work, on purpose — a reader needs the finding, not the false leads that preceded it.

What Changed

Two lines in an alerting config, and a new rule: no permissions change ships without a paired alert-path check. The whole review, start to finish, ran under nine hundred words — read completely, by everyone who needed to, on the day it went out.

Quarterly Reliability Note

Response Time Improved for Every Region Except One

The finding this piece leads with is the exception, not the average — and the column stays narrow enough that the exhibit has to earn its place.

Reliability engineeringone exhibit, one argument

Median response time fell in four of five regions after the November rollout. The one that didn't move is the entire subject of this note, because an average that improves while one region regresses is the kind of chart that gets cited in a board deck and then contradicted by a support ticket six weeks later.

Fig. 1 — Median response time by region, six weeks before and after rollout. Four lines fall together; the fifth (ap-south) holds flat.

ap-south holds flat because its traffic still routes through the older edge configuration — the rollout script skipped it due to a naming mismatch that had nothing to do with the change actually being tested.

A chart that shows an average improving is not the same claim as a chart that shows every region improving. This note exists because those two claims got confused in last quarter's summary.

What the Exhibit Doesn't Show

Fig. 1 stops at six weeks post-rollout because that's the data we're confident in; ap-south's fix ships next sprint, and the honest version of this exhibit will need a second line added, not a redrawn one.

Migration Retrospective

Three Decisions From the Migration, in the Order They Mattered

A ranked account, not a comparison table — order and emphasis carry the meaning here, which is exactly what this archetype is for.

Data platform teamthree items, one column

Of the dozen decisions made during the database migration, three are worth writing down for the next team to do this. They're ordered by how much time they saved, not alphabetically, and the first one saved the other two from being made at three in the morning.

1. Freeze reads before writes, not the other way around

The original plan froze writes first, on the assumption that stale reads were the safer failure mode. It wasn't — five services still needed to read during the freeze window, and read-your-own-writes bugs cascaded into three separate incident channels. Freezing reads first, and queuing writes instead, cost twenty extra minutes and prevented all three.

Test the rollback like you tested the migration — or you're not really rolling back, you're improvising twice.

2. Ship the rollback script before the migration script

Not tested and discarded — written, reviewed, and held in reserve. The team that skips this step is betting the migration works on the first try; this one didn't, on the second table, and the rollback shipped in four minutes because it already existed.

3. Page the engineer who wrote the schema, not the rotation

The on-call rotation reached someone at 3 a.m. who had never seen the schema in question. The fix took ninety minutes. A direct page to the schema's author, five minutes later, would have taken twelve.
