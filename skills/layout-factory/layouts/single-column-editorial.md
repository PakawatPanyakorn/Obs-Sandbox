# single-column-editorial

**Archetype:** single-column-editorial · **Family:** editorial-narrative

## Core Concept
One constrained reading column (65-75ch), generous margins, an optional drop cap on the opening paragraph. Hierarchy comes entirely from type scale and whitespace, never from boxes or a rail — margins are part of the hierarchy, not leftover space. The wider page container exists purely so an occasional margin sidenote has somewhere to live; that margin never holds anything else.

## When To Use
- Essays, opinion pieces, long-form journalism, executive briefs, narrative research reports, internal memos meant to be read start to finish, not scanned.
- Data-journalism narratives or single-finding research notes where every number needs a paragraph of interpretation alongside one exhibit at a time, never a bare stat.
- Ranked-list essays, chronological accounts, structured FAQs, or numbered findings where order and emphasis matter more than side-by-side comparison.

## When NOT To Use
- Anywhere the instinct is to break the column for a "key takeaways" box or pull-quote sidebar — restate the point in prose or promote it to a subhead instead; a bordered callout competing with the column is the tell this archetype exists to avoid.
- Content where items are genuinely parallel and meant to be compared at a glance (pricing tiers, product specs) — forcing that into sequential prose hides the comparison the reader actually needs; use a grid or table archetype instead.
- Charts that need to widen past the column "to give them room" — a finding that doesn't fit at column width should split into two paragraphs and two smaller exhibits, not stretch into a landscape figure that breaks the measure.

## Region / Component Guidance
- **masthead** (same measure as body, not a wide banner): title, dek, byline — establishes voice and scope before the column begins. The only large display type on the page; body type stays one size for the entire column so the scale contrast is front-loaded once, not distributed across boxes.
- **column** (the single reading column): drop cap, prose, subheads, one pull quote (centered italic, bounded by thin rules above/below, never wider than the column, at most one per 500-700 words), occasional ornament section breaks. The only region that ever holds the argument itself.
- **marginnote** (outside the grid, in reserved margin, optional): a citation or aside anchored to one specific paragraph. Collapses to an in-flow note directly under its paragraph below ~1000px viewport width.

## Content-Type Notes
- **textHeavy**: the column is the whole page — masthead, then unbroken prose from drop-capped opening to close, one pull quote every 500-700 words, margin sidenotes carrying citations so footnote scaffolding never has to exist.
- **chartHeavy**: one exhibit at a time, set at the column's own width and never wider, captioned like a numbered figure directly beneath the paragraph introducing it — the chart illustrates an argument already being made in text, it doesn't stand in for one.
- **cardOrListHeavy**: a list of comparable items renders as a numbered sequence inside the column — each item gets a subhead and paragraph, never a card. Order carries meaning; items are ranked by what actually matters, not alphabetized to look neutral.

## Medium Notes
- Markdown: this is markdown's native shape, needing almost no translation — H1 headline, italic byline, paragraphs as paragraphs, `>` blockquote for the pull quote, `---` for section breaks. Margin sidenotes are the one device with no default equivalent — degrade honestly to a parenthetical or `[^1]` footnote, not a broken layout.
- Print: single column at the live page's text width; print margins already do the sidenote's job, no special positioning trick needed. Drop cap and ornament section break render natively.
- Presentation: masthead becomes the title slide (kicker, headline, byline, nothing else); each subhead becomes its own one-idea slide cut to the fewest words that carry the point; pull quotes make excellent standalone quote slides; sidenotes don't survive — fold into a closing "notes" slide or drop them.

## Pairing Notes
- `wisdom` — its own design notes call for "zero ornament beyond what reason dictates," this archetype's entire philosophy stated as a theme brief; Cinzel's inscriptional caps make a dignified masthead without needing an image.
- `edo-stillwater` — restrained, formal, built for stillness; Cormorant Garamond's light italic display gives the masthead a calligraphic quality without competing with 700 words of body text.
- `harbor-clay` — the one modern-corporate register among the three, proving the archetype isn't tied to a classical page; DM Serif Display headline plus Lora body reads as a briefing memo as easily as an essay.
