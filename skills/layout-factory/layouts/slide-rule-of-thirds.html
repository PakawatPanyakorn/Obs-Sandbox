One Visual. One Idea.

The lead third gives the eye an anchor before it reads a single word. The body's two-thirds width is deliberately too narrow for five bullets — it forces one idea per slide, not a list of them.

Replaces: a centered title plus a bullet list — the single most common bad slide pattern.
The size and position of the visual third is what makes it read as "the anchor" — not decoration, not an afterthought. Use this when a slide has exactly one visual and supporting text, never when it has five equal bullets and no image at all.

When Not to Use This Archetype

A title or section-divider slide

This archetype needs real supporting text to fill its two-thirds; a title slide has none to give it. Use poster-broadside instead — full-bleed type treated as the entire visual event.

A deck needing identical margins on every slide

Rule-of-thirds is a single-slide content pattern, not a deck-wide grid system. Pair it with slide-master-deck-grid, which governs margins and safe zones across the whole deck — use both together, not one instead of the other.

Structural rule: the body never becomes a card grid

Supporting text stays a plain rule-and-whitespace list, sized by content length. The moment it turns into 3-4 identical bordered boxes, it has drifted into the uniform-grid anti-pattern this whole skill exists to prevent.

Editorial devices this archetype favors

One stat callout or short pull-quote per slide, styled boldly since it's read from a distance. Never a footnote or sidenote — a slide has no margin for either. Exhibits get a figure number and one line of interpretation, never a caption-less chart.
