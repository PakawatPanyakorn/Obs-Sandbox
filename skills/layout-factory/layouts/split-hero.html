The Concept

One Deliberate Cut Makes the Argument

A split-hero page is bisected once, unevenly, and never touched again. The ratio between the two halves is fixed regardless of how much copy the text side eventually needs.

One half — the wider one, "lead" — carries a single image, illustration, or exhibit at full bleed. The other half, "body," carries everything that has to be read: a short headline, one line of support, and a single action. There is no third zone competing for attention.

Because the visual half is visibly larger and positioned first in reading order, it reads as dominant without needing a badge, an overline, or a color change to say so — the asymmetry itself is the entire structuring device. Landing pages, product launch pages, and the cover spread of a report or deck are the classic fits: anywhere a single visual needs to do the convincing while a short, disciplined text column closes with one action.

Primary Action
Structural rule: fixed 60/40 — never 50/50, never centered
When Not to Use This Archetype

Content where nothing deserves single-zone dominance

A glossary, a directory, a reference index — nothing here should be forced into a "lead." Reach for card-catalogue-index instead, where structure comes from alignment and rules, not a size contrast between two halves.

The rule that keeps the split disciplined

The ratio is fixed — roughly 60/40 — and asserted in the grid, not eyeballed. It never drifts toward 50/50 to "balance" more copy, and neither half is ever centered within its own column.

Editorial devices this archetype favors

An eyebrow label, a single CTA, and at most one proof-point line — restraint, not decoration. No sidebars or footnotes: a hero is a threshold to cross, not reference material to browse.
