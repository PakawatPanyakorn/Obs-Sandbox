System 01 — Cols 1–5

The Grid Is the Argument

Hierarchy here is entirely a function of two variables: how many of the twelve columns a block is given, and where it starts. No color, box, or badge required.

A headline spanning five columns at 2.3rem reads as more important than one spanning five columns at 1rem — same module, different scale. A block starting at column 1 reads before a block starting at column 6, regardless of which is visually larger. Nothing is ever centered to "balance" the page; balance comes from column math, not symmetry.

Fig. 01 — Cols 6–12 — every image or chart is assigned a column span before anything else about it is decided. The split beside the headline is asymmetric on purpose — a centered layout has no argument to make.

Use this when content needs a citable, addressable structure: annual reports and technical documentation where every figure needs a column reference, transit and wayfinding systems, museum and exhibition signage, conference identity systems, and any brand system that has to scale from a business card to a building facade on the same underlying module.

12 columns. One module. Zero boxes.
When Not to Use This Archetype

Content with real emotional stakes

A memoir, a single dominant story that needs warmth, will read as cold inside a strict module. Reach for magazine-well instead — its lead's size dominance carries the emotional weight this grid deliberately withholds.

The rule that keeps it disciplined

Nothing is placed off the twelve-line grid, not even by a few pixels to "make it look better." If a photo doesn't fit its assigned columns, the photo is cropped — the grid is never bent.

Editorial devices it favors

Numbered figures and sections, flush-left sidenotes in the margin column instead of page-bottom footnotes, and hairline rules used as literal grid lines made visible — never a drop cap or a centered pull quote.

Standard 4.2 — Signage Hierarchy — Cols 1–7

Every Sign Gets One Message, Set at One of Four Sizes

The station wayfinding standard doesn't allow a fifth size, a second typeface, or a color that isn't safety-yellow or ink-black. Four sizes and two colors are enough to route hundreds of thousands of daily riders if the grid never wavers.

A gate sign is 240mm cap height because it must be legible from the paid-fare boundary, thirty meters out, in peripheral vision, at walking speed. A platform sign drops to 120mm because a rider standing on the platform is already stopped. A concourse directional is 80mm; a door-level line placard is 40mm. Nobody on the design team is permitted to pick a size because "it felt too small" — the size is assigned by sightline distance, calculated once, applied everywhere.

The same discipline governs color. Safety-yellow is reserved exclusively for platform-edge and emergency signage; using it anywhere else means it stops meaning "caution" the instant a rider needs it to. Every other message is set in ink-black on white, no exceptions, no seasonal campaign override.

Revision Log

4.2.1

Cap heights recalculated after the 2024 platform extension added 30m to average sightline distance.

4.2.0

Safety-yellow restricted to platform edge only; previously also used on service-change notices.

4.1.3

Door-level placard size reduced from 50mm to 40mm after a two-year legibility audit at walking speed.

Typeface selection follows the same logic: a single grotesque family, three weights, no italics. Ragged-right, flush-left setting was chosen specifically because justified text produces uneven word-spacing at the sizes required for peripheral legibility — a justified 240mm sign is measurably harder to scan at speed than a ragged one, so justification is disallowed system-wide, not just discouraged.

Four sizes. Two colors. No exceptions.

Study 07 — Dwell Time — Cols 1–5

Riders Read a Sign in 1.4 Seconds, Not the 4 We Designed For

An eye-tracking study measured how long riders actually looked at each sign type before making a wayfinding decision. Every design assumption before this study assumed at least three seconds of attention. None of them survived.

Fig. 07 — Cols 6–12 — median glance duration by sign type, tracked across 12,000 platform crossings. The exhibit is sized to its assigned seven columns regardless of how the data itself would "prefer" to be shown — the module decides the frame, not the chart.
