What Does Amazon Actually Do?
The Simple Story

Imagine a shopping mall so big that almost anything you can think of is inside it. But instead of just selling stuff, Amazon also rents out the computers and storage rooms that power most of the internet. Netflix, Airbnb, and even the US government keep their data on Amazon's servers. Amazon also runs ads on its website — like putting billboards in that giant mall where millions of people already go shopping every day. The more people shop, the better the ads work, which brings in more sellers, which means more stuff to buy, which brings in more shoppers. It's a loop that keeps feeding itself.

The Big Bet Right Now (2026)

Amazon is spending $200 billion this year on AI data centers, custom computer chips, and robots — more than any company in history has ever spent in a single year. The bet: AI is going to need enormous amounts of computing power for decades, and Amazon wants to be the electricity grid of the AI era. Their AWS cloud is already growing at 28%, the fastest in over three years. Meanwhile, their own AI chips (called Trainium) are now selling out — and Amazon is quietly exploring selling those chips directly to other companies, not just using them internally. That's the next act most people haven't noticed yet.

Business Model
How They Make Money
  • Online stores — selling products directly to consumers
  • Third-party marketplace fees — charging sellers a cut (typically 8–15%) to list on Amazon
  • AWS cloud services — renting servers, storage, databases, and AI compute to businesses
  • Advertising — selling sponsored product placements within Amazon search results
  • Prime subscriptions — $14.99/mo for fast delivery, Prime Video, Alexa+, and perks
  • Physical stores — Whole Foods and Amazon Fresh locations
Why the Model Is Clever
  • The flywheel: more shoppers = more sellers = better selection = more shoppers — a self-reinforcing cycle that has compounded for 30 years
  • Retail losses subsidize Prime loyalty, which locks in customers, which drives advertising revenue from sellers who need to reach those customers
  • AWS profits (13.1% operating margin) cross-subsidize retail investment, making it nearly impossible for a pure-play retailer to compete on price
  • Advertising sits on top of purchase intent — shoppers searching for "running shoes" are worth 10x more to an ad buyer than someone browsing social media
  • Custom silicon (Trainium/Graviton) cuts AWS computing costs, widening margins as AI demand scales
Hidden Catalyst Deep Dive — Amazon's Trainium Chip Business
This section covers the growth driver most analysts are not fully pricing in: Amazon's proprietary AI chip business and its potential transition from internal cost-saver to external revenue engine.
What Is the Opportunity?

Amazon builds its own computer chips (called Trainium for AI training, and Graviton for general cloud work). Right now, Amazon mostly uses these chips inside its own AWS data centers to save money — instead of buying expensive Nvidia GPUs. But here is the big secret: this chip business already generates $20 billion per year just from internal use. CEO Andy Jassy said on the Q1 2026 earnings call that if Amazon sold these chips to outside companies — like Nvidia does — the annual revenue could reach $50 billion. And he said there is "a good chance" Amazon starts doing exactly that within two years.

Why Amazon Has the Edge Here

Nvidia is brilliant at chip design, but Amazon is brilliant at system design — the whole package of chips, cooling, networking, and software that makes an AI factory work. Amazon's Trainium3 chip is already sold out. OpenAI has committed to 2 gigawatts of Trainium capacity, and Anthropic has committed to 5 gigawatts. Amazon has $225 billion in revenue commitments tied to its Trainium platform — a backlog that rivals the GDP of many countries. No other cloud company has built chips AND has a captive customer base of this scale testing them at production load for years. That experience is a moat that takes at least five years to replicate.

Competitive Edge
*
The Flywheel — 30 Years and Still Spinning
Amazon's marketplace flywheel (more sellers → more selection → more shoppers → more sellers) has compounded for three decades. No competitor has successfully disrupted it. Walmart and Alibaba have tried the hardest; both remain distant second-place.
*
AWS First-Mover Advantage in Enterprise Cloud
AWS launched in 2006 — seven years before Azure, nine before Google Cloud. That head start created deep integrations with enterprise IT stacks. Switching from AWS is expensive, painful, and slow. AWS operating margins ($14.2B income on $37.6B revenue = 37.8% margin in Q1) dwarf the retail business.
*
Advertising on Purchase Intent (Underappreciated)
Amazon's $70B+ advertising business sells ads to people who are already actively trying to buy something. This is structurally more valuable than social media ads targeting people mid-scroll. Advertisers pay a premium for this intent — and Amazon has 200 million Prime members who search before they buy.
>
Custom Silicon as a Cost Moat
By building its own Trainium and Graviton chips, Amazon cuts its GPU costs compared to buying Nvidia hardware. This lets AWS undercut competitors on price while maintaining higher margins — a structural cost advantage that compounds as AI workloads scale.
>
Logistics Network as Competitive Moat in Retail
Amazon operates one of the world's largest private logistics networks: over 1,000 warehouses, a growing fleet of aircraft, and last-mile delivery capability in most US zip codes. Building this from scratch would cost hundreds of billions and take 20+ years.
*
Alexa+ as the Consumer AI Gateway (The Underrated One)
Alexa+ is now live across ~200 million Prime households. Users are having 2–3x more conversations than with the original Alexa. Unlike ChatGPT (which has no commerce layer), Alexa+ is directly wired to Amazon's purchase ecosystem — making it the only AI assistant that can convert a conversation into a transaction in seconds.

FTC Advertising Antitrust Investigation
The FTC and multiple state AGs are drafting a formal complaint targeting Amazon's $70B advertising business, alleging misleading pricing and hidden "reserve pricing" in ad auctions. Potential for billions in civil penalties and forced operational changes to the highest-margin segment.
Active as of June 2026 · Sources: Bloomberg Law, TradingKey (Jun 22, 2026)

EU Digital Markets Act — AWS Gatekeeper Designation
European Commission released preliminary findings designating AWS as a "gatekeeper" under the EU Digital Markets Act. Final designation would require interoperability, ban lock-in practices, and carry penalties up to 10% of global annual turnover (~$72B).

Investor Debate
The Optimist Sees...
  • AWS is the most profitable cloud platform in the world, growing at its fastest rate in over 3 years, with AI just beginning to contribute meaningfully to revenue.
  • The advertising business is a $70B+ juggernaut growing 24% annually — and it mostly operates at zero marginal cost since the traffic and data already exist.
  • The Trainium chip backlog ($225B) represents multi-year locked-in revenue that de-risks the $200B capex bet — the customers have already committed before the money is fully spent.
  • Alexa+ with 200 million Prime households is a distribution channel for AI commerce that no tech company — not OpenAI, not Google — can replicate without building Amazon's entire retail infrastructure.
  • Amazon stock at $233 is trading 35% below analyst consensus of $315, implying the market is giving away the AI option for free.
The Pessimist Sees...
  • The FTC's advertising complaint targets the single highest-margin segment. Even a partial win for the government could force changes to auction mechanics that permanently impair ad revenue.
  • $200 billion in capex is an extraordinary bet. If AI demand plateaus or compute efficiency improves faster than expected (via model compression), Amazon could be left with excess capacity and massive depreciation.
  • AWS market share gains have been modest despite years of investment. Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud have closed the gap materially since 2020, and both have stronger enterprise AI software ecosystems.
  • The EU DMA gatekeeper designation for AWS could force interoperability requirements that make it easier for customers to move data off AWS — eroding the stickiness that justifies premium AWS pricing.
  • At $2.6 trillion market cap, Amazon needs to generate massive incremental earnings just to justify the current valuation — let alone deliver the upside analysts predict.
Bottom Line Verdict Editorial Opinion

Think of Amazon like a city that started as a bookstore. The bookstore became a shopping mall, the shopping mall needed a power grid, and that power grid — AWS — turned out to be worth more than the mall. Now Amazon is building its own electricity generators (Trainium chips) to power not just its own city but every city in the AI era. The question is whether the city council (regulators) lets them keep building.

The long-term case is genuinely compelling: AWS at 28% growth is accelerating, not decelerating; advertising is a near-pure-profit business with structural advantages over every competitor; and the Trainium chip business — if it reaches external sales — represents a new $30–50B revenue stream that the market has not begun to price. Prime membership with Alexa+ integration means Amazon has the only AI assistant that can convert a conversation into a delivered product within 24 hours. That is a moat no AI lab currently has.

The near-term pain is equally real. The FTC advertising complaint could land any month and will trigger immediate stock volatility — even if Amazon ultimately prevails over a multi-year legal process. The EU DMA designation adds a second regulatory front. And $200 billion in capex means free cash flow will be negative or barely positive through 2026. Investors with a 3–5 year horizon can likely look through these headwinds; investors needing near-term certainty should wait for the FTC filing to price in before building a position. The 52-week high was $278 in early 2026 — the stock has already pulled back 16% from that peak, creating a more attractive entry relative to the unchanged analyst consensus of ~$315.
