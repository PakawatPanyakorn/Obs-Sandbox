What Does This Company Actually Do?

The Simple Story

Enhanced Group does two things. First, it puts on a sports event called the Enhanced Games, where — unlike the Olympics — athletes are allowed to use performance-enhancing drugs, and the company pays out huge cash bonuses for breaking world records. Second, through a platform called Live Enhanced, it sells supplements and doctor-prescribed hormone and peptide treatments online to everyday people who want to look, feel, or perform better.

The Big Bet Right Now

The company is betting that the controversy and spectacle of the Games generates enough free media attention to build a trusted consumer brand — one that it can then use to sell its telehealth products at scale. For that bet to pay off, the Games need to keep drawing sponsors and viewers even as mainstream sports bodies keep disowning it, and the FDA needs to keep allowing the specific peptides Live Enhanced is built around.

Business Model

How They Make Money

  • Brand sponsorships tied to the annual Enhanced Games ($32M+ already committed for the 2026 debut event)
  • Media and broadcast rights as the Games franchise grows
  • Prescription revenue from Live Enhanced telehealth (TRT, HRT, longevity drugs, peptides)
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) supplement sales through the same platform

Why the Model Is Clever

  • The Games generate huge, low-cost media attention (1B+ people engaged from a "marginal media investment," per the company) that a traditional telehealth startup would have to pay dearly for
  • Sponsorship revenue is collected largely upfront, ahead of the telehealth ramp, helping fund the buildout
  • The same audience — people interested in performance and biohacking — is the exact customer Live Enhanced is trying to sell to
Hidden Catalyst Deep Dive

The Opportunity

On July 23–24, 2026, the FDA's Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC) votes on whether seven peptides — including BPC-157, Semax, and Epitalon — should be added to the legal list of substances pharmacies can compound for patients. This is the exact category of product Live Enhanced's prescription business is built around. A favorable outcome would legally de-risk the fastest-growing part of Enhanced Group's business; almost none of the retail coverage of ENHA discusses it, since it's buried under headlines about the Games' doping controversy.

Why This Company Wins

Enhanced already has the brand and the customer funnel: millions of people who watched the Games associate the company with cutting-edge performance science. If the FDA formally sanctions broader peptide access, Live Enhanced can market these products as legitimate, doctor-prescribed treatments rather than gray-market biohacking — a credibility jump that's hard for smaller telehealth peptide sellers to match without the same media reach.

Bottom Line Verdict [EDITORIAL OPINION]
The Verdict

Enhanced Group is like a carnival barker who also happens to run a pharmacy in the back room — the loud, controversial show out front draws the crowd, but the quieter business in back is what could actually pay the bills long-term.

The green case is straightforward: the Games franchise has proven it can generate outsized attention and sponsorship dollars relative to its size, and if even a fraction of that audience converts into Live Enhanced telehealth customers — especially with a favorable FDA peptide ruling in late July — the combined business could grow into today's speculative valuation and beyond.

The bear case is just as real: this is a pre-profit, recently public company whose flagship product is formally rejected by every legitimate governing body in its industry, whose telehealth revenue is still nearly invisible, and whose stock has already fallen sharply over the past year. This is a highly speculative, event-driven name — appropriate only for risk-tolerant investors who can stomach binary swings around the Jul 23–24 FDA vote and the Aug 7 earnings report, not a core holding for anyone with a multi-year, low-volatility time horizon.
