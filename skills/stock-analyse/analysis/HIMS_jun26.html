What Does This Company Actually Do?
The Simple Story

Imagine you're embarrassed to talk to a doctor about hair loss or weight gain. Hims & Hers says: "Chat with a doctor from your couch. We'll mail the medicine." They charge a monthly subscription — like Netflix, but for your health.

The Big Bet Right Now

Everyone wants the "magic weight loss shot" (semaglutide / Wegovy / Ozempic). Hims was selling a cheaper copy-cat version until the FDA said stop. Now they've switched to selling the real brand-name version in partnership with Novo Nordisk. This is their biggest make-or-break moment.

Business Model

How They Make Money

  • Subscriptions: Customers pay monthly for treatment plans. Very sticky — once you're on a medication routine, you keep paying.
  • One-time purchases: Skincare, vitamins, supplements sold without a subscription.
  • Consult fees: Small fee for the online doctor visit.
  • International: Expanded to Australia, UK, Japan via Eucalyptus acquisition.

Why the Model Is Clever

  • No insurance headaches: Patients pay directly. Simpler, faster.
  • No brick-and-mortar: No hospital buildings, just software and partner pharmacies.
  • Embarrassment factor: People happily pay online for hair loss, ED, or weight meds they'd never buy in person.
  • Lock-in effect: Once your prescription is set up, switching is a hassle.

The Peptide Play — HIMS's Next Big Bet (Often Overlooked)
What Are Peptides? (Plain Explanation)

Peptides are tiny chains of amino acids — the building blocks your body already makes. Think of them like "instruction messages" that tell your body to heal faster, build muscle, burn fat, sleep better, or age slower. They're not steroids. They're more like biological software patches. Biohackers and athletes have used them for years, but they've been hard to get legally.

Why This Is a Big Deal for HIMS

The FDA is holding a major advisory committee meeting on July 23–24, 2026 to decide whether 12 peptides can be legally compounded and prescribed. If approved, this unlocks a brand-new market estimated at $20 billion — and HIMS is the only telehealth company in America that already owns its own peptide manufacturing facility. Everyone else would be scrambling to build one; HIMS is already ready.

Bottom Line Verdict
High-Potential, High-Turbulence — Not for the Faint of Heart

Think of HIMS like a surfer who built a great surfboard and was riding the wave perfectly — then the wave (cheap GLP-1 drugs) got cut off by the government. Now they've had to quickly jump on a different, harder wave (selling the real brand-name drug). The surfing skills are real. The new wave is real. But the wipeout risk is higher than it used to be.

The long-term story is believable: digital health, direct-to-consumer, sticky subscriptions, international expansion, and the GLP-1 mega-trend are all legitimate tailwinds. The 2030 target of $6.5B revenue is not crazy for a company already at $2.35B growing 59%.

But the near-term pain is real: the DOJ investigation is unresolved, margins just got hit hard, and revenue growth slowed sharply. Analyst consensus is HOLD at $27. Stock is currently trading ~$35 — above consensus — meaning the market is already pricing in some optimism. Suitable for investors with a 3–5 year horizon who can stomach -30% drawdowns on bad regulatory news.
