What Does Oklo Actually Do?
Simple Story (10-year-old version)

Imagine a nuclear reactor small enough to fit in a single large warehouse instead of a whole city block. Oklo builds those. They call them Aurora powerhouses — they run on a special type of nuclear fuel, produce clean electricity 24 hours a day regardless of weather, and are designed to sit right next to a data center so the internet company doesn't have to worry about the power grid. Oklo builds the reactor, owns it, and sells the electricity under a long-term contract — sort of like a landlord who owns a power plant instead of an apartment building.

The Big Bet Right Now

In 2026, the world's biggest tech companies — Meta, Microsoft, Google — are racing to build massive AI data centers that need enormous amounts of reliable, clean electricity. Wind and solar don't run at night or in calm weather. Traditional nuclear plants take a decade to build. Oklo's bet is that its smaller, faster-to-deploy Aurora reactor is the right tool for that job. The company has secured a 14 gigawatt order book (enough to power roughly 10 million homes) and is racing to get its first commercial reactor approved by U.S. nuclear regulators. Everything depends on that first unit going live.

4
Recurring revenue flywheel
Each deployed reactor generates 20+ years of contracted power revenue. The more reactors deployed, the larger the annuity. This is not a one-time hardware sale.
Hidden Catalyst Deep Dive — The Tennessee Nuclear Fuel Recycling Play
The Simple Version

America's existing nuclear power plants have been generating electricity for decades — and they leave behind used fuel that is still mostly full of energy but sitting in temporary storage because no one in the private sector has ever recycled it commercially. Oklo is about to change that. The company has broken ground (metaphorically — permits still pending) on a $1.68 billion facility in Oak Ridge, Tennessee that will be the first privately funded nuclear fuel recycling plant in U.S. history. It will take that used fuel, extract the still-useful material, and forge it into fresh fuel for Oklo's own Aurora reactors. Think of it like a company that builds electric cars AND runs the battery recycling plant that feeds its own production line.

Why This Is Underreported

Most analysts covering OKLO focus on the NRC license for Aurora — the reactor approval story. Almost no one is pricing in the fuel recycling play. If successful, this facility would: (1) give Oklo a proprietary, domestic, low-cost fuel supply for its own reactors permanently insulating it from geopolitical fuel disruptions; (2) create a fuel services revenue stream that can sell recycled fuel to other advanced reactor operators (TerraPower, X-energy, etc.) who will also need HALEU (High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium) fuel; and (3) potentially qualify Oklo for DOE fuel supply contracts as U.S. policy pushes to end dependence on Russian HALEU enrichment. The Tennessee facility effectively turns Oklo from a power company into a nuclear supply chain platform — a far larger and more defensible business.

The Opportunity — Specifics
  • Location: Oak Ridge, Tennessee — 248 acres on land with deep nuclear heritage and existing NRC relationships
  • Investment: Up to $1.68 billion in total facility cost (first phase: fuel recycling; later phases: fuel manufacturing)
  • Jobs: 800+ new positions; initial hiring begins late 2026
  • Timeline: Pre-application NRC engagement ongoing; fuel production expected early 2030s
  • DOE backing: U.S. Department of Energy helped clear the Oak Ridge site, validating the location
  • Centrus LOI (June 18, 2026): Oklo also secured a supply agreement with Centrus for HALEU delivery starting 2029 — showing the fuel strategy runs on two parallel tracks simultaneously
Why Oklo Has the Advantage Here
  • Fast-fission reactors like Aurora run on metallic HALEU fuel — the same form that comes out of fuel recycling. No one else has this fuel-reactor alignment.
  • Oklo is the only private company pursuing both reactor deployment AND fuel recycling in the same decade. TerraPower and X-energy will need fuel; they are not building recycling capacity.
  • Oak Ridge location is steps away from existing DOE nuclear infrastructure — faster permitting, co-located expertise, and government site preparation already done.
  • ARMEC acquisition (June 4, 2026) adds 40 nuclear-experienced engineers and machinists in Oak Ridge — the exact city where the Tennessee facility will operate. Strategic hiring synergy.
  • Meta prepayment provides capital to advance both reactor construction AND fuel supply in parallel — competitors lack this secured funding structure.

Bottom Line Verdict

Imagine a company that has convinced a thousand restaurants to sign 20-year contracts for a new type of oven that doesn't exist yet — but the oven can cook food faster, cleaner, and cheaper than anything else, and the government just approved the safety plans for building the first prototype. That's Oklo. The order book is real. The demand from Meta and Switch is real. The physics works. The question is purely one of execution timeline.

The long-term case is credible: AI electricity demand is structural and growing, the nuclear renaissance has bipartisan political support, and Oklo has layered three business lines — reactor power, in-house manufacturing, and fuel recycling — that reinforce each other in ways no competitor has matched. If the first reactor goes online in 2029-2030 as planned, the company's valuation could look cheap at today's prices in hindsight.

The near-term pain is also real: $60.88 is 69% below the October 2025 peak, there is no revenue today, and the NRC process is genuinely unpredictable. This stock can fall another 30% if Step 4 slips to mid-2027 or if NRC issues a major request for additional information on the COLA. This is a 3-to-5-year investment minimum, sized for investors who can hold through volatility without panic. Speculators watching for a short-term squeeze have a different thesis entirely, anchored to the 20% short float.
