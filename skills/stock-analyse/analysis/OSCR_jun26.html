What Does Oscar Health Actually Do?

The Simple Story

Imagine buying health insurance the way you order an Uber — simple app, clear prices, no confusing jargon. Oscar Health built exactly that. Instead of calling a broker or drowning in a hundred-page PDF, Oscar members pick a plan on their phone, find a doctor nearby, and message their care team for free. Oscar then pays the hospitals and doctors when members need care. The government (through the ACA) helps millions of Americans pay their monthly premiums with subsidies. Oscar collects those premiums and keeps a slice after paying medical bills.

The Big Bet Right Now

In 2026, Oscar is betting that its decade of technology investment is finally paying off — and it is. For the first time ever, the company is genuinely profitable at scale. But the bigger bet is Lucie: a new AI-powered shopping marketplace launched in April 2026 that lets any American — whether they get insurance through their job, are self-employed, or are between jobs — compare and buy health coverage in minutes. If Lucie becomes the Amazon of health insurance shopping, Oscar turns from a single insurer into the rails that the entire individual health insurance market runs on.

Business Model

How They Make Money

  • Insurance Premiums: Members pay monthly; government subsidies top-up most of it via ACA Advanced Premium Tax Credits
  • Underwriting Margin: Revenue minus medical costs (claims paid to hospitals/doctors) = profit; target medical loss ratio below 85%
  • +Oscar Technology Licensing: Oscar licenses its tech platform to other health insurers serving ~500K lives outside its own membership
  • Lucie Marketplace Fees: New (April 2026) — commissions and platform fees for connecting consumers to plans, ancillary products (dental, vision, accident)
  • Risk Adjustment Transfers: CMS-run program that redistributes money between insurers based on member health risk; Oscar can be a net receiver if its members are sicker than average

Why the Model Is Clever

  • Technology as a Moat: Oscar built its own claims processing, care management, and consumer app from scratch — every other insurer buys these from vendors, so Oscar's unit costs drop as it scales
  • Direct-to-Consumer Brand: In a category where people hate their insurer, Oscar has a recognizable consumer brand — unusual and valuable
  • Data Flywheel: More members means better risk pricing, better AI models for utilization management, and lower medical costs per member
  • Network Effects via Lucie: If Lucie becomes the default comparison tool, every consumer who shops there is a potential Oscar customer — competitors pay to list on Oscar's platform
  • ACA Focus = Concentrated Expertise: By doing one market (individual ACA) better than anyone else, Oscar has skills UnitedHealth or Aetna simply don't prioritize
Hidden Catalyst Deep Dive — Lucie & the ICHRA Revolution

What Is ICHRA and Why Does It Matter?

Imagine your boss giving you a $500 monthly gift card that you can only spend on health insurance — and you get to pick whatever plan you want. That is basically what an ICHRA (Individual Coverage Health Reimbursement Arrangement) is. Instead of the company picking one insurance plan for everyone, they give each employee money and let them choose. Right now, only about 350,000 employers offer ICHRAs — but that number is projected to grow 3x to 5x by 2030, potentially moving millions of workers from group insurance onto the ACA individual market. Oscar's entire business benefits from every single one of those people.

Lucie: The Missing Shopping Layer

The problem with ICHRAs was always complexity — workers given a stipend had no easy way to compare individual market plans across carriers. Oscar launched Lucie in April 2026 to solve exactly this. Lucie is a one-stop storefront where individuals, gig workers, and ICHRA recipients can compare every major health plan (including Oscar's competitors), add dental, vision, accident coverage, and complete their enrollment in one flow using AI-guided recommendations. Oscar earns platform fees regardless of which plan the consumer picks. It is a toll-road on the entire individual market, not just Oscar's own insurance sales.

The Opportunity — Specifics

  • ICHRA market size today: ~350,000 employers; ~$2B in employer contributions annually (MODEL ESTIMATE)
  • Projected 2030 size: 3x–5x growth per industry forecasts — potentially 1–1.5 million employers
  • ACA individual market size: ~20 million enrolled today; ICHRA shift could add 5–10 million more over 5 years
  • Lucie's fee structure: Not yet disclosed; guidance expected September 2026 earnings call
  • Competitor exit tailwind: Cigna and Aetna pulling back from ACA markets; Oscar has "good overlap" in exiting markets for 2027 growth
  • Supplemental products: Lucie lists Aflac and other ancillary products, creating commission revenue beyond health insurance

Why Oscar Has the Advantage

  • Built for individual market: Oscar is the only major insurer that is 100% focused on the ACA individual market — ICHRA growth is a direct tailwind with no business model conflict
  • Technology infrastructure already built: The Lucie platform runs on Oscar's existing tech stack; marginal cost to add ICHRA routing is low
  • Consumer brand recognition: Oscar's brand is unusually strong for a health insurer — Lucie benefits from this trust
  • AI-guided enrollment: Oscar's decade of member engagement data trains the AI recommendations inside Lucie — newcomers cannot replicate this quickly
  • Network breadth: Oscar listing competitors' plans on Lucie is a strategic long-game — it builds consumer traffic that ultimately benefits Oscar enrollment while collecting platform fees
  • CEO Mark Bertolini: Former Aetna CEO who built the industry's most sophisticated consumer health platform — he has done this before

Bottom Line Verdict
The Verdict — June 2026

Think of Oscar Health like a surfer who spent 14 years paddling out through brutal waves (losses, regulatory uncertainty, failed partnerships) and has finally caught a great wave (the first profitable year). The question is not whether the wave is real — it clearly is. The question is whether a second, bigger wave (Lucie / ICHRA) is forming behind it, or whether the ocean is about to go flat (ACA market contraction). The answer to that question determines whether this stock is at the beginning of a multi-year run or already priced for perfection.

The long-term story is genuinely believable. Oscar has built something that could only be built once — a consumer brand with proprietary technology in a $500B+ industry. Lucie is a real product solving a real problem. The ICHRA market growth is secular and regulatory-driven, not cyclical. Management under Bertolini has executed the 2024–2026 turnaround better than most analysts expected. If Lucie proves it can generate meaningful platform revenue by 2027–2028, today's $8.4B valuation will look very cheap against an $18B+ revenue business with growing margins and a two-sided marketplace.

But the near-term pain is equally real. ACA subsidy expiration is not resolved, and the political path to restoration is uncertain. H2 medical costs will be much higher than Q1, testing whether the profitability story holds. The stock at $27.74 already trades 30%+ above analyst consensus, meaning the September 2026 earnings call must deliver credible Lucie guidance or risk a sharp pullback. For long-term investors with a 3–5 year horizon who can absorb ACA volatility, OSCR is a compelling story. For shorter-term holders, the risk/reward near all-time highs without clear Lucie monetization is genuinely two-sided.
