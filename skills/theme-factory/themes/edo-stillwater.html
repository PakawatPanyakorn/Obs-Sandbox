Hiroshige's Calm Shore
Edo Period Formal Composition
The Ukiyo-e tradition demands restraint. Each line serves the whole; no element exceeds its station. Calm is not the absence of form but its perfect equilibrium — achieved through discipline, not decoration. Prussian blue arrived in Japan around 1820 and Hiroshige made it stillness itself.

群青 Prussian Blue

Gunjō — the imported pigment that redefined Ukiyo-e from 1820. Hiroshige and Hokusai built entire worlds from this single cool hue on washi paper.

Flat Ink Composition

No shadows in the image space. Woodblock enforces a single flat layer — depth through arrangement and line weight, not illusion.

青碧 Celadon Register

Seiheki — the cool jade of Edo ceramics. A restrained counterpoint to the dominant blue, never louder than the ink that contains it.
